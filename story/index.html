So what are these five opportunities? Let's start with the first one, which is unusual because it's an opportunity to screw up, and you'll take advantage of it by not doing something that could cost you a lot of money.

I call it The Dreaded Salary Question, and it usually comes up early in the interview process, sometimes before you even start interviewing. It looks like this:

"So where are you right now in terms of compensation, and where do you want to be if you make this move?"

Sound familiar? I bet it does. And I bet you're thinking really hard about how you answered this question last time you were asked. "What did I say? Did I tell them?"

You shouldn't disclose your current or desired salary in a negotation—it will cost you money if you do. Don't squander this opportunity by disclosing your current or desired salary.

John didn't disclose his current or desired salaries, and look how it worked out for him...

So that's your first opportunity. What's the second opportunity to make a lot more money by doing the same work?

If you do well in your interviews, you'll get a job offer. Your next opportunity to make a lot more money is how you respond to that job offer: If you negotiate, you'll make a lot more money over the course of your career. If you just accept the job offer, you'll cost yourself a lot of money.

Justin almost passed on this opportunity, but he didn't! Instead, he took advantage of this opportunity and made $11,000 more last year.

This one is easy: They make an offer, and you counteroffer. If counter, you can make more money. If you don't counter? You can't make more money.

You should negotiate your starting salary. If you don't negotiate, you're costing yourself a lot money. Don't do that. Be like Justin!

Or Shannon!

What's next?

Even if you counteroffer, you're not done negotiating! There's still another opportunity to make a bit more money before you finalize things!

Once you counter, they'll usually come back to you with some sort of compromise—something less than your counter. This is a chance for you to keep negotiating to eke out a little more base salary and maybe even some other benefits like extra vacation, a signing bonus, or even work from home a couple days a week.

So don't stop negotiating after you counter! You could miss an opportunity to make more money and get some other benefits to boot!

Josh C. did a great job negotiating a big salary increase, but he didn't stop there! He also got an extra week of vacation each year...

{% include testimonials/josh_c.html %}

So those are three opportunities you'll have to make more money when you're moving into a new job. If you take advantage of those opportunities, you'll make a lot more money during your career.

What about when you're staying put?

There are two great opportunities to make more money at your current job. One pays off immediately, and the other will pay off later.

Let's start by looking at the longer-term payoff: getting a promotion. Most people sit back and wait for promotions to come to them. But you won't do that because you want to progress as quiclky as possible so you have more experience and a greater opportunity to get paid.

You'll actively pursue your next promotion, then your next one, and your next one so that you're always moving forward. You'll get more promotions that way, and you'll make more money that way.

And the fifth opportunity to negotiate a higher salary is getting a raise. Just like promotion, most people sit back and wait for raises to come to them. They wait for merit increases or market adjustments to be granted by HR istead of just going after them.

But you'll actively pursue raises so that you're always paid what you're worth and recognized for all the value you add to your company. All you need to do is occasionally do some market research so you know your market value, and keep track of all the new skills and experience you contribute to your company as you add more and more value.
