Here are some common reasons we talk ourselves out of asking for a raise or promotion—many of these may sound familiar:

  • I don't even know how to ask for a raise or promotion. Do I ask in person? Through email?
  • What if my manager says no?
  • Do I deserve a raise or promotion?
  • Do I just need to work harder and harder until I get a raise or promotion?
  • Don’t I have to wait until my next performance review?

Raises and promotions seem like things that are granted to us by companies when they feel like it. So it's easy to just sit back and wait for one to come to you when it's time. And sometimes you wait, and wait, and wait, and nothing seems to be happening.

A CEO describes raises and promotions from a manager's perspective

I asked the CEO of a large public company "How do you find the people that you promote to be your VPs and SVPs?" I wanted to know the secret sauce for finding untapped potential, for identifying future superstars so that I could get promoted myself, and so that I too would know how to find talent.

He said, "I look for people that are already working in and exploring areas outside their own, and I promote them." In other words, he was looking for people already doing the jobs he needed to fill.

Business managers don’t generally give raises or promote people based on potential, but based on results. They’re looking for people who have already demonstrated that they can do the job.

Managers are very, very busy people. They don’t have much time to constantly evaluate each employee's job title and pay. They barely have enough time to delegate and manage the business they’re responsible for running. So they’re not looking for potential, they’re looking for value right now.

You don't have to wait for raises and promotions to come to you.

Once you know that managers are looking for value right now, the raiase and promotion process becomes very simple:

  • Set your goal.
  • Build your case.
  • Present your case.
  • Follow up.
